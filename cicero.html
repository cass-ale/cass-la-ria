Young Cicero du Lac

Chapter I

Once, a young man would walk the length of the Lake daily outside his home.

This Lake was, and very much still is, one of the Great Lakes, and the young man was often told of the gifts one could receive—

the very house he called home was given to his grandfather by the Lake many, many years before even this story could be told. A gruff, middle-aged man limping across the sands; war had left his grandfather bereft of even the most modest of shelters. He fell face-first into the pebbled sands, too tired to even wish for an end to his aching. Seeing this, the Lake gathered dark clouds and wept, and, in its pity for the old veteran, receded its waters and produced an old house it had once reclaimed—

yet his gift had yet to come.

The young man combed the shore.

He picked up a stone, examined it, buffed it, examined it—unsatisfactory.

The Lake will want a gift in exchange for mine, he thought. What would a lake, a Great Lake, want?

He placed the stone back where he had found it.

The young man paused and thought, What will I even ask the Lake for? What do I want more than anything?

He picked up a stone, examined it, buffed it, examined it—decent.

He tossed the stone toward the Lake.

One skip . . .

Two skips . . .

Sink!

The young man had done better than a measly two skips before. A childhood friend had recounted another boy from a neighboring town that had reached 17 skips, and, as no one challenged the claim, the young man had kept the gossip as gospel in his mind with only minor lingering doubts surrounding it. Would someone really lie about such a feat?

Another stone caught his eye.

The young man picked it up, examined it, buffed it, examined it–exceptional!

He tossed the stone toward the lake.

One skip . . .

Two skips . . .

Three skips . . .

Four skips . . ?

Five skips . . !

Six skips . . .

Seven skips . . .

Eight skips . . .

Nine skips . . ? !

Swat!

The young man stood awestruck.

Swat? How could that be? Of the countless days he had combed the shore of this Lake in search of Perfect Stones to toss toward the waters, he had neither so nearly broken into the territory of double digits nor had he seen an attempt at such feats rejected by the water itself.

Ow!

The waters of the Lake began to recede, and an uncanny figure—vaguely shaped as a person who had seldom compared their form to the true thing—began to form and rise.

Ow~!, it said again, a strange and watery voice sounding like a person submerged, you have been coming here and throwing rocks at me for years! What did I do to you? Have we even met before?

The young man struggled to find words, but he eventually replied with a hesitant apology to the figure.

He couldn’t really tell where he should look. The eyeless, faceless, water-being seemed to be looking right at him, he thought.

Don’t you have friends to bother? You look far too old to be skipping stones! You look. . .

There seemed to be a moment of, at the young man’s best guess, recognition. The figure began its approach.

Ah, I see it now, The Lake said, you have the same face as that sad, old one I loved so much! Ha~! You must be in great need of something, you must. I see so in those eyes of yours. Out with it now!

The young man stammered, You are… the Great Lake?

The Lake replied, I am… the Great Lake! Do I need to work on my delivery to make that more obvious?

If the young man had had a more vivid, violent way with his words he would have cursed all fates for having brought the wish-granting Great Lake to him at this current moment, in which he struggled to put his desires to word.

Ah, the Lake sighed and extended a watery limb to the young man’s face, that face, that expression that speaks with overwhelming passion! Ha~! Speak not, gentle heart, I know exactly what you need.

You do?

Mhm~! Lean in close to me.

The Lake bowed its head slightly, and the young man, after slight hesitation, did the same.

The waters of the Lake darted forward with great speed and enveloped the young man’s head.

Blind and breathless, he struggled in vain to escape the waters’ vise grip. Amidst the roars of crashing waves and swirling timpani, the resonant voice of the Lake filled his mind:

Be still…

He stilled himself.

Open your eyes…

He opened his eyes, and could feel the water pouring inside, wrapping gently around each eyeball.

What you need is…

V I S I O N . . .

What you need is…

Something that will ease your…

L O N E L I N E S S . . .

My special gift to you…

Pretty eyes, the same color I get to wear when my favorite sky is out…

Pretty eyes, for you to find…

L O V E . . .

The young man sat up. He had been lying in the same spot for a couple of hours, perhaps. He stood, aching, and brushed off the sand and pebbles that had begun to impress their shapes into his back and shoulders. He tiredly headed toward his home.

Surely that wasn’t a dream, he thought to himself. He looked toward the sky and saw the setting sun surrounded by an expected entourage of dark reds, oranges, yellows, purples, and the encroaching navy blues. He stepped toward the water.

The last few rays of light from the retreating sun had left one last bright patch for the day. The young man walked ankle-deep into the water to get a look at his reflection before retiring for the evening.

My eyes, he mouthed.

They had changed. No longer the typical brown, but now a light gray that warned of impending rain. His encounter wasn’t a dream!

These new eyes, this vision… will help me find love? Love is all I’ll need?

He was truly happy at that moment. So happy he joyously wept all his tears into the Lake.

He returned home for the night. The young man had no further need of stone skipping nor his daily walks. He resolved himself to go into town the next day, and there, he hoped, he would lay his new eyes upon his love.

Chapter II

Once, there was a happy family of four who lived in an old house near the Great Lake, Ealasaid, as the townsfolk had recently named the Great Lake.

The two young sons of the family developed a myriad of games, only complex in their rules, mechanics, and win-conditions to the boys themselves, yet which, unsurprisingly, always found them running up and down the same path from their home to a large, grassy field a short distance away.

On this particular day, the boys were in the middle of an intense game of hide and seek. They hid, while their mother and father tried so desperately hard to seek.

The older of the two boys was a natural leader—the combination of his sharp wit and brawn solidified his authority over the other neighborhood boys. He was very protective of his younger brother, or rather he was possessive in that only he was allowed to bully his younger brother.

The younger was far scrawnier and more timid than his brother. He enjoyed being included in every game or hare-brained scheme his brother would concoct.

In that day’s game, however, he was quite confused.

Why are we hiding from Mommy and Pop?

What you mean why, the older boy heavily sighed, I’ll talk slow for you. Those two been going back and forth, back and forth from our house to the lake, out the back, every night. I know you don’t pay attention, that’s what I do. I pay attention. You know why we can’t play at the Lake?

Cause we can’t swim…

No, YOU, can’t swim. I know how. I had EJ show me how last summer—I learned it quick, too—but WE can’t go because they not gon let us try and get our gifts too soon. I’m sick and tired of them hiding from us, so we hiding from them. I wanna know why they go and we can’t.

Maybe Mommy is getting hers cause she don’t have one yet, only Pop do.

The older sighed again, heavier than the last.

You probably right. I really hate that you are. You know what she gon wish for?

I hope a bigger house.

No, dummy. She want a daughter.

A girl?! The younger boy was shocked, feeling almost embarrassed for his Mommy.

I already got you following me around outside everywhere, and if me and you got a sister, then she gon take my room while we have to share!

The older boy stood from their hiding spot, taking on a stance that matched his resolve.

Well she can keep that room, and you can keep yours. I’m leaving. I’m almost grown, and I can get people to do what I say. I know how to get money.

Leave?

Sure right. If they don’t find me by time the sun leave, then I’ll leave. They supposed to love me, right? They keep finding more and more other people to love and spend time on! They real good at that.

The younger boy’s head began to droop, defeated. He had no idea how to talk to his brother like this. He felt permanently beneath him, at a gap no birthday, no school lesson could fill.

You ain’t gotta cry. Stop with all that. This supposed to be about me.

The older boy sighed, the lightest sigh of the afternoon.

Lemme back in the hiding spot, I bet they gon be at the Lake all night anyway…

Ha~! I’m back with good words for all who’d like to hear!

The gray-eyed man gently squeezed his wife’s hand, gave her a reassuring nod, and watched as she walked toward the waters.

Your gift, my sweet, would be tricky for just little ol’ me to deliver, so I reached out to Someone Most High, the Lake said while gesturing with a watery limb upward toward the skies, and got the approval!

Goodness, thank you so much, the woman beamed.

The Lake caressed her cheek. Of course, my sweet, sweet darling~! It makes me stir with joy to see the great passion and need of beings so young. Now, I know you desired another child…

A daughter, the woman interrupted.

And while it is approved, the Lake continued, undeterred, it will not be easy. You will feel A L O N E, but know that you are not. You will be offered temptation after temptation to ease your trial, your anxiety, your L O N E L I N E S S . . . but you must not accept. You must promise me you will be strong. Failure would mean both you and the child must needs live within me, in my depths.

I would do anything for my daughter. I have never been more sure of anything like I am sure of this. So sure in fact…

Oh~? How sure?

So sure, I’ll let you know now that on the day she is born, I will bring her here and give her a name…

E A L A S A I D

The Lake seemed to smile, no one could ever be sure.

Ha~! I like-like you, sweetie. You and I are the best things to happen to Gray-Eyes, right?

Right!

The woman and the Lake both laughed.

Oh! By the way, you’ve got two kids hiding from you in the field that you told to stay home~!

Oh, do I? The woman quickly turned to her husband, Go bring them home!

The gray-eyed man nodded and headed off to the field.

On the day their daughter was due to be born, the full family walked to the Lake. The woman walked slowly, gracefully, supported by her husband and two boys.

They approached the shore, the Lake’s figure rose from the waters and approached. It bowed its head to the woman’s stomach, stood up straight, and informed them to return in a week.

Knowing better than to ask questions, they returned home.

The following week, the family arrived as they had before. The Lake was there, waiting for them. It approached, as it had before, bowed its head to the woman’s stomach, as it had before, and it stood up straight.

The Lake placed a hand on the woman’s stomach.

This child here, will…

B E A R   W A T E R . . .

Calm yourselves…

Stand up straight to meet your daughter…

She who will be a…

W A T E R - B E A R E R . . .

The Lake lovingly and gently lifted the newborn into its embrace. It then handed the child to the mother.

The gray-eyed man wept joyous tears.

The two young boys stood in awe.

The mother’s face bore nothing but concern.

This child, she spoke softly, my daughter… you have handed me a…

This child is a boy…

Oh~? It is a boy, isn’t it? Maybe waiting a week was too long after all…
