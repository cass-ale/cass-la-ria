# Chinese Translation Theory — Key Figures and Philosophies
Source: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_translation_theory

## Ancient Foundations (3rd-7th century AD)

### Confucius (attributed)
"Names should follow their bearers, while things should follow China" (名從主人，物從中國)
— Names should be transliterated; things should be translated by meaning.
**Relevance:** This is the earliest articulation of the foreignize/domesticate split.

### Kumārajīva (344–413 AD)
Translated for MEANING, not words. Criticized literal translations as "chewing up rice and feeding it to people" (嚼飯與人) — the nutrition is there but the beauty is destroyed.
**Relevance:** The earliest argument for dynamic equivalence — 1,500 years before Nida.

### Huiyuan (334–416 AD)
The "Middle Way" of translation: avoid extremes of elegant (文雅) and plain (質樸).
- Elegant translation: "the language goes beyond the meaning" (文過其意)
- Plain translation: "the thought surpasses the wording" (理勝其辭)
- Good translation: "the words should not harm the meaning" (文不害意)
**Relevance:** Balance between beauty and fidelity — the central tension in all translation.

### Xuanzang (600–664 AD)
Established the Five Untranslatables (五種不翻) — categories of words that should NOT be translated:
1. Secret words (dharani/mantras)
2. Polysemous words
3. Words for things not existing in China
4. Words with established transliterations
5. Words whose meaning is enhanced by transliteration
**Relevance:** Directly applicable to brand names and culturally specific terms.

## Modern Chinese Translation Theory

### Yan Fu (1898) — 信達雅 (Xin Da Ya)
The most influential Chinese translation theory. Three principles:
- **信 Xin (Faithfulness):** Accuracy to the source meaning
- **達 Da (Expressiveness/Fluency):** Smooth, natural target language
- **雅 Ya (Elegance):** Literary beauty and refinement

Yan Fu wrote: "Faithfulness is hard enough. But without expressiveness, the translation is useless. Hence besides faithfulness and expressiveness, I also aim at elegance."

**Relevance:** The Ya (elegance) criterion is what elevates translation from functional to artful. This is the Chinese equivalent of what Cotter calls "force" and what Rabassa achieves intuitively.

### Lin Yutang (1933)
Three criteria for translation: faithfulness (忠實), fluency (通順), and beauty (美).
Lin was a self-translator (wrote in both Chinese and English). He argued that the translator must have:
1. Deep understanding of the source language
2. Mastery of the target language
3. A sense of aesthetics that bridges both cultures

His "translation aesthetics" emphasized that translation is not just linguistic but CULTURAL — the translator must understand the aesthetic sensibilities of both cultures.

### Lu Xun (1935)
Advocated "hard translation" (硬譯) — deliberately foreignizing, preserving the strangeness of the source text. He believed translation should EXPAND the target language, introducing new structures and ways of thinking.

Opposed to smooth domestication. His position: "Rather be faithful than smooth."

**Relevance:** The opposite of Rabassa's approach. Lu Xun would keep foreign syntax to enrich Chinese. For brand/creative text, this maps to preserving the foreignness of an artist's identity.

### Fu Lei (1951) — 神似 (Spiritual Resemblance)
Fu Lei's theory of "spiritual resemblance" (神似) over "formal resemblance" (形似):
- **形似 (Formal resemblance):** The translation looks like the original (word-for-word structure)
- **神似 (Spiritual resemblance):** The translation FEELS like the original (captures the spirit)

Fu Lei drew this from Chinese painting theory — a portrait that captures the subject's spirit is superior to one that merely copies their features.

**Relevance:** This is the Chinese equivalent of Nida's dynamic equivalence, but expressed through the lens of Chinese aesthetics. For creative text, always aim for 神似.

### Qian Zhongshu (1964) — 化境 (Sublimation)
The highest level of translation is 化境 (huajing) — "sublimation" or "transformation into a new realm."

At this level, the translation doesn't read as a translation at all. It reads as if it were originally written in the target language. The translator has so deeply internalized both the source and target cultures that the result transcends both.

Qian wrote: "The highest standard of literary translation is 'transformation' (化). The translated work enters the target language so completely that it doesn't read as a translation."

**Relevance:** This is the gold standard. When translating "Multi-faceted creative — music, video, and visual art" into Chinese, the result should feel like it was originally conceived in Chinese.
