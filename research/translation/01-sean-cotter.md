# Sean Cotter — Translation Philosophy & Method

Source: Words Without Borders, "Romanian Translation's Johnny Appleseed: An Interview with Sean Cotter" by Clara Burghelea, Nov 30, 2022
URL: https://wordswithoutborders.org/read/article/2022-11/romanian-translations-johnny-appleseed-an-interview-with-sean-cotter-clara-burghelea/

## Key Findings

### Process
- Writes **three drafts** before sending the fourth to the press
- Collaborates with the original author (Cărtărescu) via rounds of queries
- Queries address not just details but **interpretations**: "is this ironic here, is this supposed to be grotesque"
- Collaborates with translators of the same work in other languages (e.g., Laure Hinckel, French translator)
- Deep cultural immersion — lived in Romania, returns frequently, walks through Bucharest

### Philosophy
- Translation as **rewriting in another language** — not mechanical substitution
- The intensity of the prose "demanded my attention" — translation requires total immersion
- Different authors require different approaches: "Is there a different approach when works diverge in style?"
- Cotter was "surprised by the number of themes they share" between different Romanian authors — finds connections across works
- Translation as cultural agency: "Before publication, the translator is often the work's agent"

### Academic Work
- Author of "Literary Translation and the Idea of a Minor Romania" (University of Rochester Press, 2014)
- Professor at University of Texas at Dallas, Center for Translation Studies
- National Endowment for the Arts Translation Fellow
- International Booker Prize longlisted translator

### Key Translations
- Mircea Cărtărescu, *Blinding* (Archipelago Books, 2013)
- Mircea Cărtărescu, *Solenoid* (Deep Vellum, 2022)
- Nichita Stănescu, *Wheel with a Single Spoke and Other Poems*
- Magda Cârneci, *FEM* (Deep Vellum, 2021)

## Arts Fuse Critical Review — "Translucent Translations" (2012)
Source: The Arts Fuse, by Ellen Elias-Bursac, September 26, 2012
URL: https://artsfuse.org/69764/fuse-poetry-review-translucent-translations-wheel-with-a-single-spoke/

### Cotter's Core Translation Principle: "Translucence"
Cotter's own words on his approach:
> "The translated text seems transparent, in that it promises us another poetry, and yet it is merely translucent, in that we see that poetry's light while noting our distance from the experience of its words. The task I set myself in translation is to maintain, as much as possible, that sense of the halo around the poems."

This is the KEY insight: Cotter does NOT aim for transparency (making the translation invisible) nor opacity (making the foreignness dominate). He aims for **translucence** — you see the light of the original shining through, but you're aware you're looking through a medium.

### Specific Techniques Praised by Reviewer
1. **Sound architecture in English** — builds assonance, rhyme, and music that may or may not mirror the original but creates its own sonic coherence
   - Example: "kiss/yes/bistro/as if" — internal rhyme chain
   - Example: "you see/leaf/green/screams/feel/tree" — consonance positioning

2. **Word-play preservation** — In "Knot" (Stănescu's last published poem):
   > "I thought of a way so sweet / for words to meet / that below, blooms bloomed / and above grass greened. // I thought of a way so sweet / for words to smash / that perhaps grass would bloom / and blooms would grass."
   The verb/noun inversion ("blooms would grass") recreates the original's grammatical play in English.

3. **Register matching** — Stănescu's poetry ranges from philosophical to childlike. Cotter matches each register rather than flattening into a single voice.

### Reviewer's Assessment
Ellen Elias-Bursac (herself a translator) calls these "insightful translations" and praises the "wide-ranging array" that captures 20+ years of Stănescu's evolution. The reviewer specifically notes that Cotter's translations create their own music in English while honoring the original's intent.
