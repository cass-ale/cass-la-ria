# Translation Theory Research

## 1. Domestication vs Foreignization (Venuti, 1995)
Source: Wikipedia, "Domestication and foreignization"; Venuti, *The Translator's Invisibility* (1995)

**Domestication:** Making text conform to the target culture. May lose source-text information but reads naturally. The reader feels "at home."

**Foreignization:** Retaining source-text information and cultural markers. Deliberately breaks target-language conventions. The reader is "sent abroad."

**Schleiermacher's Two Paths (1813):** Either the translator moves the reader to the author, or moves the author to the reader. Cannot do both simultaneously.

**For our use case (artist website):** We need a HYBRID approach:
- **Foreignize** the artist name "Cass la Ria" — keep it untranslated in all languages (it IS the brand)
- **Domesticate** functional text (Contact, social platform names) — these must be instantly understood
- **Transcreate** any poetic/evocative text — preserve the emotional force, not the literal words

## 2. Formal vs Dynamic Equivalence (Nida, 1964)
Source: Wikipedia, "Dynamic and formal equivalence"; Nida, *Toward a Science of Translating* (1964)

**Formal equivalence:** Focus on the message itself — form and content. Poetry-to-poetry, metaphor-to-metaphor. Preserves structure.

**Dynamic equivalence:** Focus on the EFFECT on the reader. The translation should produce the same response in the target audience as the original did in the source audience.

**For our use case:** Dynamic equivalence is the right model. The site text is minimal and evocative — what matters is the FEELING it creates, not the literal words.

## 3. Transcreation vs Translation (Industry Practice)
Source: Lokalise, "Transcreation vs. translation: What's the difference, really?" (2025)

**Translation:** Straight-across transfer. Keep facts, tone, and order intact. Not word-for-word, but sense-for-sense. Best for: documentation, product specs, legal text.

**Transcreation:** Alchemy. Words rebuilt to spark the same emotional response in every culture. Starts with a creative brief, not a source text. Best for: ads, slogans, taglines, brand voice, creative copy.

Key insight from Lokalise: "The ad demands alchemy, words rebuilt so they spark the same laugh or surge of confidence in every culture."

**For our use case:** The Cass la Ria site text is 100% creative/brand content — this is a TRANSCREATION task, not a translation task.

## 4. Synthesis: The Right Approach for Cass la Ria

The site contains approximately 5 translatable text elements:
1. "CASS LA RIA" — artist name (NEVER translate)
2. "Contact" — functional CTA
3. Email address — functional (NEVER translate)
4. Social platform labels (if any) — functional
5. Any future tagline/bio text — creative/poetic

**Decision matrix:**
| Text Type | Approach | Theory Basis |
|-----------|----------|-------------|
| Artist name | Foreignize (keep as-is) | Venuti: preserve the foreign |
| Functional UI | Domesticate (natural target language) | Nida: dynamic equivalence |
| Creative/poetic | Transcreate (rebuild for same force) | Crucefix: preserve the force, not the words |
| Cultural references | Adapt with care | Bonnefoy: empathize with the original intention |
