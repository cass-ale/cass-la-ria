# Martyn Crucefix — Twenty Pointers to Translating Poetry

Source: Martyn Crucefix blog, January 30, 2018
URL: https://martyncrucefix.com/2018/01/30/twenty-pointers-to-translating-poetry/

## The 20 Pointers (Condensed)

1. Can we translate a poem? The safe answer is "No — too much will be lost." But...
2. Ignore that crushing absolutism. Just do it. "Some sports are painful, and their labour / Delight in them sets off."
3. To translate is to incur guilt — the moralistic tone proves the importance and passionate intimacies involved.
4. Define translation broadly (1): responding to emoji is translation. It's not merely language-to-language but period-to-period, register-to-register, formal-to-vernacular.
5. Define translation broadly (2): Any good poem is a form of translation. Tranströmer saw poems as "manifestations of invisible poems written beyond languages themselves." Rita Dove: translators understand that any poem is "merely a silhouette."
6. Turn the original into something in the target language **with the same information and with the same force**.
7. Use these methods (David Bellos):
   - **Synonymy** — word for word replacement (literal)
   - **Expansion** — replacement of problematic words with longer versions
   - **Contraction** — elision, skipping, turning a blind eye
   - **Topic Shifting** — rearranging sequence for clarity
   - **Change of Emphasis** — making parts stand out differently
   - **Clarification** — adding expressions not in the original, making implicit explicit
8. Poetry is poetry, so translation is mostly a question of **force** — shades, emotional colours, rhetorical temperature, ramifications of meaning.
9. Force is what Robert Frost called **"the sound of sense"** — poetry's ineffable tones, gestures, interrelations, patterns. To convey it, match such constituents (though not necessarily preserve them like lifeless bones).
10. **Triangulation** — determine direction via multiple reference points: the text itself, literary interpretation, wider cultural perspectives, the author's wider oeuvre.
11. **Empathise and keep ego quiet** — work hard to imagine what motivated the poem, re-live the act which gave rise to it (Yves Bonnefoy).
12. Measure success not by term-for-term resemblance but by **the ontological necessity of your new words/forms/images**.
13. Translation IS possible if we regard it as "poetry re-begun" (Bonnefoy).
14. Charles Tomlinson: preserve not the metre, but **the movement** of each poem — its flight, or track through the mind.
15. Michael Hofmann: **Close the source text** once your translation gains height in its flight.
16. Don't confuse translation with versioning — translation has responsibility to BOTH the source text AND a working English poem equally.
17. Ask how you'd want YOUR poems treated — translation or version?
18. Peter Robinson: versions result in failures of tone, impoverish and lower the tone. But radical revisions might equally result in a better poem.
19. Label versions and translations appropriately — responsibility to the public.
20. Translation is important because it connects cultures, preserves voices, and expands the boundaries of what language can do.

## Key Principles for Our Skill

The most applicable principles for translating short creative website text:

1. **Force over literalism** (#6, #8, #9) — preserve the emotional impact, not just the dictionary meaning
2. **Triangulation** (#10) — consider the brand identity, the visual context, the cultural associations
3. **Movement over metre** (#14) — preserve the rhythm and flow, not the exact syllable count
4. **Close the source** (#15) — once you have the meaning, write naturally in the target language
5. **Empathy** (#11) — understand what the text is trying to DO, not just what it SAYS
6. **Ontological necessity** (#12) — every word in the translation must feel inevitable, not arbitrary
