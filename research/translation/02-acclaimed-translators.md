# Acclaimed Literary Translators — Philosophies & Techniques

Source: Literary Hub, "10 Literary Translators on the Art of Translation" by Emily Temple, Nov 28, 2018
URL: https://lithub.com/10-literary-translators-on-the-art-of-translation/

## Lara Vergnaud (French→English)
Translator of Mohand Fellag, Joy Sorman, Scholastique Mukasonga

Key principle: **"Something Else but Still the Same"** (Charlotte Mandell's phrase)
- "You must capture the voice of an author writing in one language and bear it into another, yet leave faint trace that the transfer ever took place."
- Immersion is "inevitable" and "required"
- "Plunge deep" tactics: reconstruct the author's cultural touchstones (books, film, music)
- Read passages aloud in original AND translation "until hoarseness sets in"
- Animate through senses: "using my nose, my ears, my eyes, and my fingers"
- Imprint the RANGE of the author's voice: "humor, anger, grief, detachment"

## Jhumpa Lahiri (Italian→English)
Translator of Domenico Starnone's novels

Key principle: **Translation as visceral absorption**
- "Translation goes beyond reading; the act is visceral as opposed to merely intimate"
- "I have the great privilege of scrutinizing the very mechanism of his novels, of probing the structure and supporting tissue"
- Translation is "more pleasurable than writing fiction" because of the "intense relationship with a text I profoundly admire"
- "Writing is a sustained monologue... Translation is a dialogue"

## Vladimir Nabokov (Russian→English)
Translator of Pushkin's Eugene Onegin

Key principle: **Three requirements for ideal translation**
1. "He must have as much talent, or at least the same kind of talent, as the author he chooses"
2. "He must know thoroughly the two nations and the two languages involved and be perfectly acquainted with all details relating to his author's manner and methods; also, with the social background of words, their fashions, history and period associations"
3. "He must possess the gift of mimicry and be able to act, as it were, the real author's part by impersonating his tricks of demeanor and speech, his ways and his mind, with the utmost degree of verisimilitude"

Three grades of evil in translation:
- Ignorance (forgivable)
- Intentional skipping of difficult passages (worse)
- "Planishing and patting" a masterpiece to conform to audience prejudices (worst — "a crime")

## Ann Goldstein (Italian→English)
Translator of Elena Ferrante's Neapolitan Novels

Key principle: **Know the ending to get the beginning right**
- "You have to know what happens at the end to be true to the beginning in terms of the language"
- "It's not a question of individual words. You're not going to translate the same words the same way all the time"
- Preserves structural features (run-on sentences) because "they serve a purpose and they're certainly there in the Italian"
- "You're constantly second-guessing yourself and at a certain point it loses its usefulness"

## Idra Novey (Portuguese→English)
Translator of Clarice Lispector's The Passion According to G.H.

Key principle: **Translation as transformation, not substitution**
- "Translation begins with the prefix 'trans' for a reason. Like transcendence and transformation, it requires an acceptance of progressing with uncertainty"
- "In a translation, playing around with style is everything. The author provides the plot and characters"
- The translator's art is in the STYLE — the how, not the what

## Booker Prize — The Art of Translation (2022)
Source: The Booker Prizes, "The art of translation and why it matters" by Sarah Shaffi, May 28, 2022
URL: https://thebookerprizes.com/the-booker-library/features/the-art-of-translation-and-why-it-matters

Key insight from translator Frank Wynne: "Mostly it requires you to listen very intently to the voice that you can hear in the text and recreate that voice in your own language."

Key insight from McDowell: "With that depth of connection, translation moves from simply translating the words, to translating the poetry, the music, the rhythm that the author has painstakingly injected into their work. When that level of immersion can be achieved, that is when magic happens."

Key insight from Rocco: Great translated works (Tolstoy, Proust, Chekhov, Mann) become so embedded in our literary consciousness that "we don't think of them primarily as translated fiction; we take their translation for granted and almost think of them as English."

## Gregory Rabassa — One Hundred Years of Solitude
Source: Reddit r/books, multiple discussions; JSTOR analysis
- García Márquez famously declared that Rabassa's English translation was "better than the Spanish original"
- Rabassa's approach: read the entire work first, then translate in one continuous flow to maintain the rhythm
- His translation preserved the magical realism tone — the matter-of-fact narration of impossible events
- Key technique: maintaining the long, flowing sentence structures that are natural in Spanish but unusual in English — he didn't break them up

## Emily Wilson — The Odyssey (2017)
Source: The Boar, "Poetic and revolutionary" (Apr 2025); America Magazine interview (Dec 2023); Reddit r/ClassicalEducation
URL: https://theboar.org/2025/04/poetic-and-revolutionary-why-emily-wilsons-translations-of-homers-epics-are-my-favourite/

Why Wilson's translation is revolutionary:
- First woman to translate The Odyssey into English (in 3,000 years of the poem's existence)
- Uses **iambic pentameter** — same line count as the original Greek dactylic hexameter, maintaining the poem's rhythm
- Eschews "epic" elevated language — uses clear, direct, modern English
- "Wilson chooses her words very carefully to create a rhythm which mirrors the original purpose of The Odyssey as a poem, meant to be memorised by bards"
- Praised for **accessibility** without dumbing down — makes Homer feel alive, not museum-piece
- Her famous opening: "Tell me about a complicated man" vs. Fagles' "Sing to me of the man, Muse" vs. Lattimore's "Tell me, Muse, of the man of many ways"
- Each word choice is a philosophical and aesthetic decision
