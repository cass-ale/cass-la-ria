## Anton Hur — Philosophy

Anton Hur is one of the few full-time Korean-to-English literary translators in the world. Twice longlisted for the 2022 International Booker Prize (Cursed Bunny, Love in the Big City).

### Key Principles:

1. **"The most important thing is the language. They have to be good writers. Translating good writing is easy."**
   - Good writing has an oral quality — "I have to hear it."
   - Kyung-sook Shin "does not use fancy words or complicated sentence structures" yet "her prose in Korean has a rhythm to it that rocks you into your deep emotions."
   - "It's very difficult to decode but it's very clear that she is writing to me in absolute silence."

2. **Translation as subconscious process:**
   - "I always knew my subconscious was how I translated because I can't explain how I came to translate one sentence into another. It surfaces from the depths. The machine is down there and it's like a black box that I can't figure out."

3. **No need to consult the author:**
   - "I get such a visceral feeling when I read their books. I understand what they're saying and I don't need the author to explain anything to me."
   - "Kyung-sook Shin is a god of Korean literature. She doesn't need to explain herself."

4. **Writing and translation are the same process:**
   - Influenced by Lee Seong-bok's Indeterminate Inflorescence: "Each word has another word coming out after and you can sense the shape of that word. You have to write down the sounds no matter how absurd they are."
   - "If you are sensitive, you can hear the words that are trying to come out of your subconscious."

### Translation Principle for the Skill:
When translating INTO Korean, the text must have an oral quality — it should sound natural when read aloud. Korean literary prose values rhythm and emotional resonance over complexity. Simplicity is not weakness; it is mastery.

## Korean Aesthetic Concepts for Translation

### Han (恨) — Deep Sorrow / Unresolved Grief
- A uniquely Korean emotion — not quite sadness, not quite resentment
- The accumulated weight of historical suffering (colonization, war, division)
- Han Kang won the 2024 Nobel Prize for "intense poetic prose that confronts historical traumas" — her work is saturated with han
- Translation principle: Korean text should carry emotional weight without melodrama. Han is quiet, persistent, dignified.

### Jeong (情) — Deep Affection / Emotional Bond
- Interpersonal warmth that builds over time — deeper than friendship, not romantic
- The glue of Korean social relationships
- Translation principle: Korean text should feel warm and connected, not cold or distant. Even formal text should carry an undertone of human warmth.

### Sijo (시조) — Traditional Korean Poetic Form
- Three-line form, typically 44-46 syllables total
- Structure: introduction (line 1), development (line 2), twist/resolution (line 3)
- The third line MUST contain a "twist" — a surprise, a reversal, a new perspective
- More ancient than haiku, originating in the Goryeo period (918-1392)
- Explores cosmological, metaphysical, or pastoral themes
- Translation principle: Korean text benefits from a three-part structure with a surprising or resonant conclusion. The ending should reframe what came before.
